# Abstract

This paper revises and formalizes a claim in the Lambda / TUFT / NSAF working corpus: named entities, physical objects, attractors, and shared observations do not arrive as absolute self-bounded units independent of scale, metric, threshold, and comparison. They appear as stable-enough cuts through a field of relations. This does not deny constraint, recurrence, matter, or invariance. It denies that the nameable object, as nameable, can be separated from the measurement and registry conditions that make it available as an object.

The term "physical" therefore should not function as an escape hatch from taxonomy. A physical entity is not a non-taxonomic object standing outside all frames. It is a highly constrained, repeatable, intersubjectively stabilizable registry of relations. Physical description becomes powerful precisely because its cuts can be disciplined by measurement, transformation, prediction, instrumentation, and shared correction.

The attractor case gives a rigorous exemplar. A strange attractor is a bounded invariant structure generated by deterministic causal rules whose iteration produces long-horizon unpredictability, sensitive dependence, recurrence, folding, and often fractal dimension. It is a simple rule producing complex distribution. The attractor itself is not a random cloud. It is the registered support of recurrence under an evolution rule.

The central theorem in this paper states that every compact attractor of a finite-dimensional smooth flow or discrete map can be represented as a localized invariant set inside a common measurement architecture. Through embedding, normalization, and phase-coordinate lifting, any such attractor can be placed into a sphere and compared through Hopf-type phase coordinates. This does not claim that every native dynamical system is secretly a Hopf fibration. It claims that every compact attractor, once available as a persistent invariant set, is mappable into a shared nested phase-sensitive registry. That is the precise formal sense in which the attractor examples generalize.

The Thomas, Halvorsen, Aizawa, Lorenz, Roessler, Chua/double-scroll, Henon, logistic, Ikeda, Duffing, double pendulum, standard map, Baker map, Smale horseshoe, toroidal/quasiperiodic systems, and cymatic/Chladni pattern analogs are treated as exemplars of one structural fact: simple rules plus iteration, boundary, feedback, projection, and measurement can produce richly organized forms without requiring the form to possess an absolute pre-given skin.

# 1. Ontological posture: constraint without naive object-boundary realism

The argument begins with a distinction.

1. **Constraint is real.** Recurrence, resistance, invariance, transformation law, measurable effect, and shared correction all matter. A framework that denies constraint collapses into empty subjectivism.
2. **Named boundaries are relational.** A thing becomes available as a named entity only under a metric, scale, threshold, coordinate frame, persistence criterion, or comparison procedure. A framework that denies this collapses into naive object-boundary realism.
3. **The physical is not outside this distinction.** "Physical" names the class of relations whose constraints remain stable under disciplined measurement and transformation. It does not name a magical registry-free object.

Thus, when one says that an atom lacks an absolute skin, the claim does not mean that atoms are imaginary. It means that the boundary called an atomic radius depends on the operative registry: covalent radius, van der Waals radius, ionic radius, electron-density isosurface, scattering behavior, bonding relation, or another measurement convention. The constraint persists; the named boundary depends on the cut.

The same pattern appears in attractors. A Lorenz attractor has a determinate equation and a recognizable invariant set. Yet the plotted butterfly is also a measurement event: a projection of recurrence into a chosen coordinate representation, resolution, parameter regime, and visualization threshold. The attractor has mathematical identity, but the visible form is still a registered form.

# 2. Comparative frames and measurement cuts

A **comparative frame** is a tuple

\[
F_{\mathrm{frame}}=(S,M,d,\mu,\mathcal{O},s,\varepsilon,\sim),
\]

where:

- \(S\) is the source domain or state space.
- \(M\) is a measurement or representation space.
- \(d\) is a metric or distinguishability relation on \(M\).
- \(\mu\) is a measure, weight, or density rule when relevant.
- \(\mathcal{O}:S\to M\) is an observation, chart, projection, or encoding map.
- \(s\) is a scale or resolution parameter.
- \(\varepsilon\) is a threshold of distinction.
- \(\sim\) is an equivalence relation declaring which differences count as the same registered object.

A **measurement cut** is a stabilized subset

\[
E_{F}=\{x\in S:\rho_s(\mathcal{O}(x))\ge \varepsilon\}/\sim,
\]

where \(\rho_s\) may denote density, recurrence strength, energy, probability, semantic intensity, measurement confidence, or another frame-specific quantity.

This definition separates three layers:

1. **Underlying constraint:** something produces recurrent or measurable effects.
2. **Measurement cut:** a frame selects what counts as bounded or nameable.
3. **Registry identity:** a community, agent, instrument, or formal system treats the cut as a repeatable entity.

The named object is not "made up." It is a stabilized cut through constraint.

# 3. Dynamical systems and attractors

Let \(M\) be a metric space. A continuous-time dynamical system is a flow

\[
\Phi^t:M\to M,
\qquad
\Phi^{t+u}=\Phi^t\circ\Phi^u,
\qquad
\Phi^0=\operatorname{id}.
\]

A discrete-time dynamical system is an iterated map

\[
F:M\to M,
\qquad
x_{n+1}=F(x_n).
\]

A compact set \(A\subset M\) is an **attractor** when it satisfies three core conditions:

1. **Invariance**
   \[
   \Phi^t(A)=A
   \quad\text{for all }t
   \]
   in the flow case, or
   \[
   F(A)=A
   \]
   in the map case.

2. **Attracting neighborhood**  
   There exists an open neighborhood \(U\supset A\) such that
   \[
   \lim_{t\to\infty}\operatorname{dist}(\Phi^t(x),A)=0
   \quad\text{for all }x\in U,
   \]
   or
   \[
   \lim_{n\to\infty}\operatorname{dist}(F^n(x),A)=0
   \quad\text{for all }x\in U
   \]
   in discrete time.

3. **Persistent identity under resolution**  
   The set remains distinguishable under a chosen observational resolution. This third condition is not always stated in pure dynamical systems language, but it becomes necessary when the attractor is used as a named form or compared across systems.

A **strange attractor** typically combines attracting behavior with nontrivial internal dynamics: sensitive dependence on initial conditions, stretching and folding, dense or recurrent orbits, fractal or non-integer dimension, positive Lyapunov exponent, positive entropy, or related complexity measures. Different authors emphasize different subsets of these features. The present framework does not require a single rigid definition of "strange." It requires only that a persistent invariant set can hold complex recurrence within bounded support.

# 4. Simple causal rules and bounded unboundedness

A strange attractor displays a precise kind of bounded unboundedness.

It is **bounded** in state space: the trajectory remains trapped in a finite region or compact invariant set.

It is **unbounded in effective descriptive complexity**: as observation time, resolution, and symbolic coding depth increase, the number of distinguishable orbit segments, recurrence relations, and local distinctions can continue to grow.

Thus the phrase "unbounded complexity inside bounded structure" should not mean that magnitudes become unbounded. It means that a bounded invariant support can encode indefinitely refinable distinctions through iteration.

A deterministic rule can therefore produce unpredictability without randomness. If two initial states satisfy

\[
d(x_0,y_0)=\delta,
\]

then for a system with positive Lyapunov exponent \(\lambda>0\), separation may grow approximately as

\[
d(\Phi^t(x_0),\Phi^t(y_0))\approx \delta e^{\lambda t}
\]

until boundedness forces folding or saturation. Prediction fails at long horizons because finite precision in the initial state becomes amplified, not because the rule ceases to be deterministic.

This gives the primitive structural pattern:

\[
\text{simple rule}+\text{iteration}+\text{feedback}+\text{boundedness}
\longrightarrow
\text{complex recurrent distribution}.
\]

# 5. The universal attractor mappability theorem

The universal claim must be stated exactly. It should not say that every attractor has the same native equation, the same physical cause, or the same visible shape. It should say that every compact attractor can be placed into a common representational architecture that preserves its invariant identity up to the chosen equivalence.

## Theorem 1: Compact attractor representation

Let \(A\) be a compact attractor of a finite-dimensional smooth flow or finite-dimensional discrete map. Then \(A\) admits a faithful topological representation as a compact invariant subset of some Euclidean measurement space \(\mathbb{R}^{N}\). If the state space is a smooth \(m\)-manifold, Whitney-type embedding gives such a representation in finite dimension. If \(A\) is a compact metric set with finite covering dimension \(k\), Menger-Noebeling-type embedding gives an embedding into \(\mathbb{R}^{2k+1}\).

Let

\[
\iota:A\hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{N}
\]

be an embedding. Then the induced evolution on \(\iota(A)\),

\[
\widetilde{\Phi}^{t}
=
\iota\circ \Phi^t\circ \iota^{-1},
\]

or

\[
\widetilde{F}
=
\iota\circ F\circ \iota^{-1},
\]

is topologically conjugate to the original evolution restricted to \(A\). Therefore recurrence, invariance, orbit relation, and dynamical identity are preserved.

### Proof

By hypothesis, \(A\) is compact and belongs to a finite-dimensional smooth or finite-dimensional metric setting. Standard embedding theorems place such sets into finite-dimensional Euclidean measurement spaces under the stated hypotheses. Since \(\iota\) is a homeomorphism onto its image, every orbit in \(A\) corresponds uniquely to an orbit in \(\iota(A)\). The pushed-forward dynamics preserve composition, invariance, recurrence, and attracting identity. Therefore the attractor is representable as a localized invariant subset in a common measurement space. \(\square\)

## Interpretation

This theorem proves the mappability claim in the appropriate mathematical sense. It does not make a new physical discovery. It names a structural fact already implicit in dynamical systems: an attractor, once treated as a compact invariant object, can be transported into another representation while preserving its dynamical identity.

# 6. Hopf-type phase representation

The Hopf layer adds a common phase-sensitive registry. It must be stated carefully.

## Theorem 2: Spherical and Hopf-coordinate lifting

Let \(A\subset \mathbb{R}^{N}\) be a compact represented attractor. Define the spherical lift

\[
J(x)=\frac{(x,1)}{\sqrt{1+\|x\|^2}}.
\]

Then

\[
J:A\to S^{N}\subset \mathbb{R}^{N+1}
\]

is an embedding. By placing \(\mathbb{R}^{N+1}\) inside \(\mathbb{C}^{n+1}\) for sufficiently large \(n\), \(J(A)\) becomes a compact subset of

\[
S^{2n+1}\subset \mathbb{C}^{n+1}.
\]

The Hopf fibration

\[
S^1\longrightarrow S^{2n+1}\xrightarrow{\pi}\mathbb{CP}^{n}
\]

then supplies a phase-fiber coordinate structure. The lifted attractor \(J(A)\) can be analyzed through total-space coordinates in \(S^{2n+1}\), base coordinates in \(\mathbb{CP}^{n}\), and local fiber phase \(S^1\) where appropriate.

### Important qualification

The Hopf projection \(\pi\) may identify points along a common phase fiber. Therefore the base projection \(\pi(J(A))\) alone need not preserve every distinction unless the embedding has been chosen to avoid unwanted fiber collapse or unless fiber phase is retained. The faithful representation lies in the total lifted coordinate \(J(A)\subset S^{2n+1}\), with Hopf projection used as a structured comparison map.

## Interpretation

This proves the precise TUFT/NSAF-compatible claim:

> Every compact finite-dimensional attractor can be lifted into a nested spherical phase registry in which Hopf-type fiber/base decomposition becomes available for comparison.

It does not say that every attractor is natively generated by Hopf dynamics. It says that every such attractor can be represented in a Hopf-compatible measurement architecture.

# 7. Attractors as semi-resonant persistences

A **semi-resonant persistence** is a compact invariant attracting structure that remains recognizable under a measurement registry while retaining internal unresolved variation.

This definition unifies several cases:

- **Fixed point:** recurrence collapses to a stable state.
- **Limit cycle:** recurrence repeats periodically.
- **Torus:** recurrence becomes quasiperiodic across incommensurate angular coordinates.
- **Strange attractor:** recurrence stretches, folds, and sensitively depends on initial conditions while remaining bounded.
- **Symbolic attractor:** recurrence appears as constrained sequences under a shift or grammar.
- **Pattern-forming mode:** recurrence or standing structure appears under boundary conditions and excitation.

The point is not that all these cases have identical dynamics. The point is that each becomes a named form through persistence, recurrence, and a measurement cut.

# 8. Exemplars of the construction

The following examples show the claim across continuous flows, discrete maps, forced systems, symbolic systems, and physical pattern analogs.

## 8.1 Lorenz attractor

\[
\dot{x}=\sigma(y-x),
\qquad
\dot{y}=x(\rho-z)-y,
\qquad
\dot{z}=xy-\beta z.
\]

The Lorenz system is the canonical example of deterministic chaos in a three-dimensional flow. Its rule is simple: three coupled differential equations. Its behavior is complex: trajectories stretch, fold, switch between lobes, and become sensitive to initial conditions. The butterfly form is a bounded recurrent distribution, not a random scatter.

In the present registry, the two lobes mark recurrent regions under a projection of the invariant set. They are meaningful forms, but not absolute object-boundaries.

## 8.2 Roessler attractor

\[
\dot{x}=-y-z,
\qquad
\dot{y}=x+ay,
\qquad
\dot{z}=b+z(x-c).
\]

The Roessler system gives a spiral sheet with reinjection. It is useful because it visually clarifies how near-periodic rotation can be folded into chaos. Its form displays a transition from smooth cyclic recurrence toward strange recurrence.

## 8.3 Thomas attractor

\[
\dot{x}=\sin(y)-bx,
\qquad
\dot{y}=\sin(z)-by,
\qquad
\dot{z}=\sin(x)-bz.
\]

The Thomas system uses cyclic sinusoidal coupling with linear damping. It exemplifies how simple trigonometric feedback can generate labyrinthine bounded recurrence. In the NSAF language, it serves as a clean cyclic-coupling exemplar.

## 8.4 Halvorsen attractor

\[
\dot{x}=-ax-4y-4z-y^2,
\]

\[
\dot{y}=-ay-4z-4x-z^2,
\]

\[
\dot{z}=-az-4x-4y-x^2.
\]

The Halvorsen attractor uses cyclic negative coupling, damping, and quadratic terms. Its symmetry makes it useful for comparing how repeated algebraic relations distribute trajectories into a bounded folded form.

## 8.5 Aizawa attractor

\[
\dot{x}=(z-b)x-dy,
\]

\[
\dot{y}=dx+(z-b)y,
\]

\[
\dot{z}=c+az-\frac{z^3}{3}-(x^2+y^2)(1+ez)+fz x^3.
\]

The Aizawa attractor shows a sphere-like body with twisting internal flow. It is an especially strong example for the measurement-cut thesis because it appears visually entity-like while retaining a fuzzy, dynamic, recurrence-defined boundary.

## 8.6 Chua circuit and double-scroll attractor

A common Chua form is

\[
\dot{x}=\alpha(y-x-f(x)),
\qquad
\dot{y}=x-y+z,
\qquad
\dot{z}=-\beta y,
\]

where \(f(x)\) is a piecewise nonlinear response. The double-scroll attractor demonstrates how piecewise nonlinearity and switching can create two recurrent lobes. It supplies a physical-electronic exemplar of deterministic rules producing chaotic bounded form.

## 8.7 Henon map

\[
x_{n+1}=1-a x_n^2+y_n,
\qquad
y_{n+1}=b x_n.
\]

The Henon map is a discrete-time two-dimensional system. It shows that strange-attractor structure does not require continuous flow. Iteration alone can produce a fractal invariant set. It is one of the cleanest examples of simple algebraic update rules producing a complex registered distribution.

## 8.8 Logistic map

\[
x_{n+1}=r x_n(1-x_n).
\]

The logistic map shows period-doubling, chaos, and sensitive dependence in one dimension. Its importance for this paper is primitive: a single recurrence rule, iterated, can move from fixed point to periodic cycles to chaotic behavior as the parameter \(r\) changes. This supports the claim that complexity does not require complicated causal law.

## 8.9 Ikeda map

\[
x_{n+1}=1+u(x_n\cos t_n-y_n\sin t_n),
\]

\[
y_{n+1}=u(x_n\sin t_n+y_n\cos t_n),
\]

with

\[
t_n=a-\frac{b}{1+x_n^2+y_n^2}.
\]

The Ikeda map is phase-sensitive and naturally fits the Hopf/phase-registry intuition. The update rule combines rotation, nonlinear phase shift, and damping. It demonstrates how angular feedback can generate intricate bounded distributions.

## 8.10 Duffing oscillator

A forced Duffing oscillator can be written as

\[
\ddot{x}+\delta\dot{x}+\alpha x+\beta x^3=\gamma\cos(\omega t).
\]

By rewriting it as a first-order system and including forcing phase, one obtains a phase-space system capable of periodic, quasiperiodic, and chaotic response. Duffing shows how forcing, nonlinearity, damping, and phase combine to produce rich attractor structure.

## 8.11 Double pendulum phase space

The double pendulum is a mechanical system with deterministic equations and strong sensitivity to initial conditions. Its phase space contains regions of regular and chaotic behavior. It is important because it gives an intuitive physical exemplar: simple linked rotational relations produce complex bounded motion.

A double pendulum trajectory is not always an attractor in the dissipative sense unless damping or driving conditions create attracting sets. Its value here lies in showing deterministic sensitivity and phase-space complexity.

## 8.12 Standard map

The standard map is commonly written

\[
p_{n+1}=p_n+K\sin(\theta_n),
\]

\[
\theta_{n+1}=\theta_n+p_{n+1}\pmod{2\pi}.
\]

It is area-preserving and does not fit the dissipative-attractor template in the same way as Lorenz or Henon. Yet it belongs in the comparative registry because it shows how simple iterative angular rules generate islands, chaotic seas, resonance zones, and complex phase portraits. It demonstrates that "attractor-like visual complexity" and "dissipative attractor" should not be carelessly conflated.

## 8.13 Baker map and Smale horseshoe

The Baker map and Smale horseshoe provide symbolic models of stretching, folding, and reinjection. Their importance is structural. They isolate the primitive operation behind many strange attractors:

\[
\text{stretch} \rightarrow \text{fold} \rightarrow \text{reinsert}.
\]

This operation produces symbolic dynamics and exponential growth in distinguishable itineraries. It supplies the formal bridge between simple deterministic maps and unbounded symbolic complexity.

## 8.14 Toroidal and quasiperiodic systems

A flow on a torus may be written in angular coordinates as

\[
\theta_i(t)=\theta_i(0)+\omega_i t \pmod{2\pi}.
\]

If the frequency ratios are rational, the orbit closes. If the ratios are irrational, the orbit can densely wind on the torus. Such systems are not strange attractors by default, because they need not show sensitive dependence or fractal dimension. But they are essential comparative structures because they show how bounded phase recurrence can generate complex coverage patterns without randomness.

This links naturally to Hopf, toroidal, and nested-fiber language.

## 8.15 Cymatics and Chladni figures

Cymatic and Chladni patterns should be treated carefully. They are not automatically strange attractors. They are physical pattern-formation analogs: simple wave equations, boundary conditions, and sinusoidal excitation produce intricate nodal structures.

For a vibrating membrane or plate, a simplified modal equation has the form

\[
\nabla^2 u + k^2 u = 0
\]

under boundary conditions. The visible pattern appears where material accumulates along nodal or low-motion regions. This supports the broader thesis:

\[
\text{simple rule}+\text{boundary condition}+\text{excitation}
\longrightarrow
\text{complex registered pattern}.
\]

Cymatics therefore belongs in the paper as an analog of measurement-cut patterning and resonance, not as a default example of strange-attractor chaos.

# 9. Prime, primitive, and co-prime recurrence

The earlier phrase "prime resonance" should be formalized so that it does not claim too much.

There are three relevant meanings.

## 9.1 Primitive periodic orbits

In dynamical systems, a periodic orbit of period \(n\) is **primitive** if it is not merely the repetition of a shorter orbit. That is, \(F^n(x)=x\), but \(F^k(x)\ne x\) for \(0<k<n\).

Primitive cycles are irreducible recurrence units. They function like prime factors in the orbit registry, even when \(n\) is not a prime number.

## 9.2 Co-prime phase cycles

For coupled cyclic processes with periods \(m\) and \(n\), full realignment occurs at

\[
\operatorname{lcm}(m,n).
\]

If

\[
\gcd(m,n)=1,
\]

then

\[
\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)=mn,
\]

which maximizes the return length relative to the two cycle lengths. Thus co-prime cycles delay total coincidence and produce richer coverage before repetition. Prime periods are an important special case because primes have no nontrivial divisors.

## 9.3 Prime numbers as registry markers

Prime numbers may be used as markers of irreducible cycle length, non-coincidence, and factorization structure. The rigorous claim is not that all attractor stability is literally mediated by number-theoretic primes. The rigorous claim is:

> Primitive and co-prime recurrence classes provide natural irreducible labels for phase non-coincidence and delayed realignment in cyclic or symbolic dynamics.

This preserves the insight without overstating it.

# 10. The 1/f bridge with exact scope

A 1/f-type spectrum should also be stated with exact scope.

Suppose a signal is a superposition of relaxation modes with Lorentzian spectra

\[
S_\tau(f)=\frac{C\tau}{1+(2\pi f\tau)^2}.
\]

If the density of timescales is approximately log-uniform,

\[
g(\tau)\propto \frac{1}{\tau}
\quad
\text{for }
\tau\in[\tau_{\min},T],
\]

then the aggregate spectrum is

\[
S(f)=\int_{\tau_{\min}}^T
\frac{C\tau}{1+(2\pi f\tau)^2}
g(\tau)\,d\tau.
\]

Substituting \(g(\tau)=K/\tau\) gives

\[
S(f)=CK\int_{\tau_{\min}}^T
\frac{1}{1+(2\pi f\tau)^2}\,d\tau.
\]

With the change of variables \(u=2\pi f\tau\),

\[
S(f)=\frac{CK}{2\pi f}
\int_{2\pi f\tau_{\min}}^{2\pi fT}
\frac{1}{1+u^2}\,du.
\]

In the intermediate band

\[
\frac{1}{T}\ll f\ll \frac{1}{\tau_{\min}},
\]

the integral approaches an approximately constant value, yielding

\[
S(f)\propto \frac{1}{f}.
\]

Thus the rigorous claim is:

> When a measurement seam samples across a log-uniform hierarchy of unresolved timescales, a 1/f spectrum appears over the corresponding intermediate frequency band.

This supports the Lambda bridge without requiring every attractor or every boundary to produce 1/f noise. The observed scope remains broad, but the theorem has conditions.

# 11. Inter-agent accounting and shared reference

Let Agent A and Agent B have observation maps

\[
\mathcal{O}_A:S\to M_A,
\qquad
\mathcal{O}_B:S\to M_B.
\]

A shared perturbation \(\Psi\in S\) becomes

\[
\Psi_A=\mathcal{O}_A(\Psi),
\qquad
\Psi_B=\mathcal{O}_B(\Psi).
\]

Perfect inter-agent identity would require a map

\[
\Phi:M_A\to M_B
\]

such that

\[
\Psi_B=\Phi(\Psi_A)
\]

for all relevant \(\Psi\), while preserving the relevant metric, semantic, dynamical, and contextual structure.

In practice, the best available relation often has the form

\[
d_B(\Psi_B,\Phi(\Psi_A))\le \varepsilon,
\]

or

\[
\Psi_B=\Phi(\Psi_A)+\delta_{\text{res}},
\]

where \(\delta_{\text{res}}\) denotes translation residue: difference introduced by scale, context, non-invertibility, noise, hidden variables, semantic mismatch, or distinct registry history.

This is not a denial of communication. It is a more exact account of communication. Shared reference succeeds by stabilizing toleranced correspondences, protocols, transformations, calibration procedures, and mutual correction. It does not require proof that two agents possess an absolutely identical apprehension.

# 15. Final statement
