# Abstract

The repeating geometric pattern called the Dougherty Set by Buddy James can be read as more than a background for later artworks. At pixel level, it presents a compact incidence grammar built from repeated centers, nested circles, tangent and overlapping neighborhoods, orthogonal and diagonal propagation families, multiple cycle systems, scale changes, central concentration, and planar-to-helical lifts. This paper treats that repeating pattern as a mathematical glyph and constructs a Rosetta-style cross-reference among three registers: (1) structures explicitly developed in Randall Dougherty's work, (2) the Lambda-Nielsen-Semita Attractor Framework-Topological Unified Field Theory canon, and (3) standard mathematical realizations in graph theory, geometric topology, symbolic dynamics, coding theory, matroid theory, measure theory, and discrete geometry.

The strongest direct correlations concern Abelian Cayley graphs, degree-diameter optimization, distributed loop networks, lattice coverings, product spaces, refinability, matroidal dependence, network coding, non-Shannon information inequalities, field-dependent rank constraints, orbit equivalence, and recursively generated critical structure. The glyph does not prove these results and need not have been designed to encode them. It nevertheless preserves several primitive relations from which such structures are conventionally built. The central proposal is interpretive: one recurrent incidence grammar supports multiple mathematically coherent projections. In the Lambda-NSAF-TUFT canon, TUFT supplies an adequate relational registry, NSAF supplies dynamical selection and recurrence, and Lambda records residue between non-equivalent projections. The paper concludes with a complete Rosetta matrix, explicit limits, and a proposed program for exact reconstruction from cleaner source images.

# 5. Rosetta I: the glyph as an Abelian Cayley generator

## 5.1 Visible relation

The strongest visible structure is an orthogonal cross-lattice with repeated units along both axes. The same local motif appears under translation and approximate quarter-turn rotation.

A minimal model uses

\[
V=\mathbb Z^2,
\qquad
S_0=\{\pm e_1,\pm e_2\},
\]

with

\[
e_1=(1,0),
\qquad
e_2=(0,1).
\]

The diagonal families suggest an enriched generator set

\[
S=
\{\pm e_1,\pm e_2,
\pm(e_1+e_2),
\pm(e_1-e_2)\}.
\]

The corresponding infinite graph is the Cayley graph

\[
\operatorname{Cay}(\mathbb Z^2,S).
\]

A finite periodic quotient is

\[
\operatorname{Cay}(\mathbb Z^2/L,S),
\]

where \(L\subset\mathbb Z^2\) is a full-rank sublattice.

## 5.2 Randall Dougherty register

Dougherty and Faber studied the degree-diameter problem for Cayley graphs of Abelian groups. They explicitly relate the graph problem to efficient lattice coverings of Euclidean space by octahedra and tetrahedra, cite the two-generator case as part of distributed loop-network literature, and compare their graphs favorably with traditional toroidal meshes while retaining routing and constructibility properties [1].

The glyph's visible axial and diagonal ports are exactly the local data from which an Abelian Cayley graph is defined: a group and a generating set. The pattern therefore correlates not just with a generic grid but with the generator-level specification of a graph family.

## 5.3 Canon register

TUFT reads the generator set as a finite instruction selecting pathways inside a larger registry. NSAF reads it dynamically as a transition alphabet. Lambda appears when different generator sets or quotient lattices preserve some local relations while producing globally non-isomorphic routing structures.

For two descriptions \((\Gamma,S)\) and \((\Gamma',S')\), a residue can be associated with the failure of a candidate map \(F\) to preserve graph distance:

\[
\Lambda_d(F)
=
\sup_{x,y}
\left|
 d_S(x,y)-d_{S'}(F(x),F(y))
\right|.
\]

## 5.4 Standard geometry register

The standard realizations include periodic graphs, crystallographic actions, graph quotients, toroidal meshes, voltage graphs, and lattice networks.

**Status:** A-C strong; D interpretive.

# 6. Rosetta II: nested circles as graph balls and covering bodies

## 6.1 Visible relation

Repeated centers support several circles of different radii. Their overlaps create a hierarchy of neighborhoods.

In a graph \(G\), the radius-\(r\) ball around \(v\) is

\[
B_G(v,r)
=
\{u\in V:d_G(u,v)\le r\}.
\]

In a geometric realization, the analogous family is

\[
B(c_i,r_k)
=
\{x:\|x-c_i\|\le r_k\}.
\]

The glyph may therefore be read as drawing several reachable neighborhoods at once.

## 6.2 Randall Dougherty register

In the degree-diameter problem, one seeks a graph with as many vertices as possible subject to bounded degree and diameter. For a Cayley graph, the growth of the word-metric ball is central. Dougherty and Faber's correspondence between Abelian Cayley graphs and lattice coverings turns the graph ball into a covering body. In two dimensions, standard generators produce diamond-shaped word balls; richer generator sets produce different polyhedral neighborhoods [1].

The repeated nested circles in the glyph correlate with this family of reachable or covering neighborhoods, even though the visible circles are Euclidean rather than exact word-metric polytopes.

## 6.3 Canon register

A finite-radius neighborhood is a partial witness of the total registry. The conditional uncertainty of the exterior given the visible ball provides a Lambda-style quantity:

\[
\Lambda_r(v)
=
H\bigl(X_{V\setminus B_G(v,r)}\mid X_{B_G(v,r)}\bigr).
\]

NSAF uses expanding neighborhoods to organize propagation and attractor basins. TUFT retains the global relation structure beyond every finite radius.

## 6.4 Standard geometry register

Relevant structures include graph balls, Minkowski functionals, covering radii, Čech filtrations, Vietoris-Rips filtrations, and persistent homology.

**Status:** A-B exact at the level of nested neighborhoods; C strong by degree-diameter and covering themes; D interpretive.

# 7. Rosetta III: tangency, kissing, overlap, and the nerve

## 7.1 Visible relation

Small cells appear in chains of contact. Larger circles overlap neighboring circles, producing repeated lenses and higher-order intersections.

For a family \(\mathcal U=\{U_i\}\), define the intersection graph

\[
i\sim j
\iff
U_i\cap U_j\neq\varnothing.
\]

The full overlap structure is the nerve

\[
\mathcal N(\mathcal U)
=
\left\{
J\subseteq I:
\bigcap_{j\in J}U_j\neq\varnothing
\right\}.
\]

The contact graph retains pairwise adjacency; the nerve retains higher-order overlap.

## 7.2 Randall Dougherty register

Dougherty's direct connection here lies more in covering geometry and code neighborhoods than in classical Euclidean kissing-number theory. The same centers can be interpreted as lattice points, graph vertices, codewords, or covering centers. Pairwise touching or overlap determines nearest-neighbor and coverage relations. The degree-diameter/lattice-covering work makes this reading directly pertinent [1].

## 7.3 Canon register

This geometry visibly negotiates between a discrete L-frame of centers and edges and a continuous C-frame of overlapping regions. Reducing the nerve to its one-skeleton loses higher-order intersection information. Define

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{nerve}}
=
\sum_{k\ge2}
\beta_k\bigl(\mathcal N(\mathcal U)\bigr)
\]

as one possible record of higher-dimensional information not present in the contact graph alone.

## 7.4 Standard geometry register

The standard structures are sphere and circle packings, contact graphs, intersection graphs, nerves, Helly-type systems, Čech complexes, and Delaunay-Voronoi duality.

**Status:** A visible; B exact as a contact/nerve model; C direct through covering, indirect through packing; D strong.

# 9. Rosetta V: junctions as local network-coding kernels

## 9.1 Visible relation

At many crossings, and especially near the center, several incoming and outgoing continuations coexist. The glyph records more than a single path assignment.

A coding node takes inputs \(X_1,\ldots,X_m\) and produces

\[

\]

A linear code restricts this to

\[
Y=\sum_{i=1}^m a_iX_i.

## 9.2 Randall Dougherty register

Dougherty, Freiling, and Zeger established that linear coding can be insufficient for network information flow and developed matroidal and nonlinear approaches to solvability and rate regions [7-9,12]. The repeated junction in the glyph therefore correlates with a repeated local coding kernel. Its global behavior depends not only on adjacency but on the class of functions permitted at each node.

A representation gap may be written as

\[
\Lambda_{\mathrm{lin}}(N)
=
C_{\mathrm{nonlin}}(N)-C_{\mathrm{lin}}(N).
\]

When positive, the gap gives a rigorous example of a restricted frame failing to exhaust the network's global capability.

## 9.3 Canon register

This is among the clearest Lambda correspondences. The carrier is the full network; the restricted frame is linear coding over a selected alphabet; the residue is the unattained capacity or rate region. NSAF reads repeated coding nodes as coupled local transformations whose global orbit may stabilize, branch, or fail. TUFT stores the full relational network before a coding operator is chosen.

**Status:** A visible as multivalent junctions; B formalizable; C direct; D strong.

# 10. Rosetta VI: non-Shannon constraints and local-to-global obstruction

## 10.1 Visible relation

The glyph contains many locally coherent overlaps and paths. Yet local coherence does not guarantee that every possible assignment of states to the full grid can coexist globally.

## 10.2 Randall Dougherty register

For random variables \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\), define the entropy vector

\[
h=
\bigl(H(X_S)\bigr)_{\varnothing\ne S\subseteq[n]}.

Shannon-type inequalities follow from

\[
I(A;B\mid C)\ge0.
\]

For four or more variables, these do not characterize the entropic region. Dougherty, Freiling, and Zeger developed many non-Shannon inequalities, infinite families, auxiliary-variable methods, and applications to network capacity, including the Vámos network [10]. Thus

\[
\overline{\Gamma_n^*}
\subsetneq
\Gamma_n
\qquad(n\ge4).
\]

A candidate rank or entropy assignment can satisfy local polymatroidal rules while failing global entropic realizability.

## 10.3 Canon register

The structural statement is exact:

\[
\text{local admissibility}
\nRightarrow
\text{global realizability}.
\]

This is a domain-specific realization of Lambda residue. The visible glyph can be treated as the syntax of allowed local intersections; entropy inequalities test part of its global semantics.

## 10.4 Standard geometry register

**Status:** A indirect but relevant; B-C exact after variables are assigned; D strong.

# 12. Rosetta VIII: refinability and recursive subdivision

## 12.1 Visible relation

Smaller circles and cells appear inside interstitial spaces generated by larger elements. The system thereby appears to reuse its own admissible relation at more than one scale.

Let \(\Sigma\) be a substitution operator:

\[
G_{n+1}=\Sigma(G_n).
\]

An additive invariant \(I\) is scale-refinable if

\[
I(C)=\sum_{j=1}^m I(C_j)
\]

for an admissible subdivision \(C=C_1\cup\cdots\cup C_m\).

## 12.2 Randall Dougherty register

In the Bernoulli-measure work, refinability is an algebraic condition allowing the value of a clopen set to be decomposed into compatible smaller clopen values. Dougherty and collaborators use refinability to connect binomial equivalence with topological equivalence of product measures [2-5].

The visual correlate is not an assertion that circles are probabilities. It is the repeated principle that a larger admissible unit can be replaced by smaller admissible units while preserving a governing value or class.

## 12.3 Canon register

The distinction between finite stage and completed recursive registry is central to the author's prior inverse-limit work. A direct system records constructed stages,

\[
G_0\to G_1\to G_2\to\cdots,
\]

while an inverse system records compatible histories,

\[
\cdots\to X_2\to X_1\to X_0.
\]

Lambda records information omitted by any one finite stage. NSAF supplies the recurrence or attractor process. TUFT supplies a registry capable of carrying the full refinement hierarchy.

## 12.4 Standard geometry register

Related objects include iterated function systems, substitution tilings, graph-directed fractals, Bratteli-Vershik systems, renormalization, direct limits, and inverse limits.

**Status:** A visible as scale recurrence; B-C structurally strong; D strong.

# 14. Rosetta X: orbit equivalence and hyperfinite approximation

## 14.1 Visible relation

Repeated cells are equivalent under translations, rotations, reflections, and possibly scale substitutions. These transformations partition the grid into orbit classes.

Let a countable group \(\Gamma\) act on a standard Borel realization \(X\). Define

\[
xE_\Gamma y
\iff
\exists g\in\Gamma\quad g\cdot x=y.
\]

If

\[
E=\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}E_n
\]

with each \(E_n\) a finite Borel equivalence relation, then \(E\) is hyperfinite.

## 14.2 Randall Dougherty register

Dougherty, Jackson, and Kechris studied orbit equivalence relations induced by a single Borel automorphism and classified important aperiodic nonsmooth hyperfinite relations through Borel embeddability and invariant ergodic measures [6]. The glyph's repeated transformations offer a concrete geometric source of orbit relations.

The correlation becomes rigorous after the transformation group and measurable state space are specified. It does not depend on interpreting visible layers as literal foliations.

## 14.3 Canon register

TUFT stores orbit classes and their possible representatives. NSAF selects trajectories within an orbit structure. Lambda appears when no canonical selector survives or when a projection identifies distinct orbits.

## 14.4 Standard geometry register

Standard relatives include group actions, dynamical equivalence relations, tiling spaces, holonomy groupoids, foliated groupoids, and Borel reducibility.

**Status:** A visible as repeated symmetry; B formalizable; C direct once an action is fixed; D strong.

# Appendix A. Compact definitions

## A.1 Cayley graph

For a group \(\Gamma\) and generating set \(S\),

\[
\operatorname{Cay}(\Gamma,S)
\]

has vertex set \(\Gamma\) and edges \(g\to gs\) for \(s\in S\).

## A.2 Graphic matroid

For a graph \(G=(V,E)\), the independent sets are forests. Circuits are simple cycles.

## A.3 Entropy vector

For \(n\) random variables,

\[
h=(H(X_S))_{\varnothing\ne S\subseteq[n]}.
\]

## A.4 Hyperfinite Borel equivalence relation

A Borel equivalence relation \(E\) is hyperfinite when it is an increasing union of finite Borel equivalence relations.

## A.5 Inverse limit

For spaces and bonding maps \(p_n:X_{n+1}\to X_n\),

\[
\varprojlim(X_n,p_n)
=
\{(x_0,x_1,\ldots):p_n(x_{n+1})=x_n\}.
\]

## A.6 Nerve

For \(\mathcal U=\{U_i\}\), the nerve contains a simplex \(J\) exactly when the corresponding sets have nonempty total intersection.

## A.7 Mapping torus

For \(f:X\to X\),

\[
M_f
=
X\times[0,1]/(x,1)\sim(f(x),0).
\]
