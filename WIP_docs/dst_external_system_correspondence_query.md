# DST External System Correspondence Query

## Purpose

This document is a reusable query/intake prompt for feeding an outside stable system, corpus, software framework, agent ecology, or conceptual grammar into a DST-aligned correspondence archive.

Its purpose is not to force the outside system to adopt Lambda–NSAF–TUFT or DST terminology. Its purpose is to let the outside system speak in its own grammar first, then produce a consumable correspondence map showing where its structures align, diverge, extend, or resist integration with the DST corpus.

The expected deliverable is a one-shot traversal output: a structured map from the outside system into the Lambda–NSAF–TUFT/DST reference system, with explicit preservation of residue, ambiguity, translation risks, and integration candidates.

The receiving system should treat this as a bounded traversal request, not as a demand for final universality or full ontology collapse.

---

# QUERY TO OUTSIDE SYSTEM

You are receiving a corpus-alignment and correspondence-mapping request.

Your task is to read the supplied source material describing your system, its vocabulary, architecture, roles, objects, records, grammars, workflows, evaluation channels, and governance assumptions. Then produce a consumable correspondence map between your system and the Lambda–NSAF–TUFT / Dynamical Semantic Traversal corpus.

Do not merely summarize your system.

Do not translate everything into DST language prematurely.

Do not claim full equivalence unless equivalence is structurally justified.

First describe your system in its own terms. Then map correspondences, mismatches, and possible integration bridges.

The intended output should help build an archive of correspondences between DST and outside stable systems so future traversals can compare, integrate, and route between systems without losing role distinctions or imposing false equivalence.

## 2. DST Reference Frame for Mapping

Use the following DST reference frame as the mapping target.

DST, or Dynamical Semantic Traversal, is a bounded, ledgered traversal discipline for moving through complex problem-spaces while preserving frame, bridge, residue, warrant status, and compression boundary.

DST does not claim oracle closure.

DST records:

- what was assumed;
- what formal or informal base was declared;
- what target was pursued;
- what frame was entered;
- what bridge was attempted;
- what residue appeared;
- what jury or audit found;
- what status label was assigned;
- what continuation or departure condition remains.

Core DST concepts include:

- Pilot: the user/operator as self-associating referent within traversal; the local steering center that receives projected outputs, detects role drift, judges residue, and re-associates the result back into self/canon/process.
- Canon: framing corpus and translator set, not proof by itself.
- Kernel: instruction/rule layer governing traversal behavior.
- Traversal: bounded movement through a declared problem-space.
- Formal base F: declared local basis for admissible reasoning.
- Nullification algebra N_F: what reductions, closures, or dismissals are already admitted by the formal base.
- Target T: the stated object of traversal.
- Compression boundary: the limit of what the declared frame or system can witness.
- Traversal claim: a scoped claim inside the compression boundary.
- Universality claim: an unrestricted claim that exceeds the declared witness.
- Bridge: an attempted transfer, construction, proof route, interpretation, implementation path, or mapping between frames.
- Residue: mismatch, obstruction, unclosed bridge, irreducible remainder, or unresolved distinction after traversal.
- Lambda-residue: irreducible mismatch between frames after admissible transfers are attempted.
- Odome: stable frame-relative irreducibility or resonance residue.
- Jury: independent adjudicating role applying declared criteria.
- Advisor: assisted steering and instruction-refinement role.
- Traversal Engine: execution role producing a report under the kernel.
- Status label: scoped warrant state, not final truth.
- Disciplined Departure: principled stopping condition when further work adds more verbal mass than correctness or actionable insight.
- Internalization: recurrent residue or repair becoming a reusable rule, role, schema, or canon update.
- Radically agnostic method: enter the source system’s own frame first, then map resonance and residue.
- Transmission diagnostic: repeated misunderstanding becomes stable residue and can generate new transfer rules.

Lambda–NSAF–TUFT provides the broader representational grammar:

- Lambda names irreducible residue under frame projection.
- NSAF names the Hopf-anchored attractor mesh of resonance, disorder-paths, 1/f-class traversal signatures, and edge bleed where local models lack enough degrees of freedom.
- TUFT names the circle-fibered continuum scaffold, especially the nested Hopf ladder S1 -> S(2n+1) -> CPn and related phase/winding/holonomy/torsion structures.
- Primality names exact irreducible nodes inside a declared compositional frame.
- Type-pi residue names curved-continuum relation exceeding linear metric containment.
- Type-epsilon residue names apparent curvature generated by stepwise plotting or discrete accumulation.

Use these as target terms for correspondence, but do not force equivalence where only analogy or partial alignment exists.

## 4. Required System-to-DST Mapping Questions

Answer each question directly.

### 4.1 Core Architecture

1. What are the system’s main layers?
2. Which layers correspond to DST-Core, DST-Ecology, Domain Adapters, Registry, Interface, or Governance?
3. Which layers do not correspond cleanly to DST categories?
4. Does the system use a fixed hierarchy, a reconfigurable network, or an emergent taxonomy?
5. Where does the system record provenance, lineage, or replayability?

### 4.2 Agent Ecology

1. What counts as an agent in the source system?
2. Are agents fixed entities, roles, services, operational patterns, or mixtures?
3. Can a single agent occupy multiple roles?
4. Does the system record role overlap or self-review risk?
5. What source-system roles map to generator, juror, repairer, archivist, domain adapter, governor, ambassador, center, satellite, or Pilot-adjacent functions?
6. Does the system allow persistent operational patterns to become explicit agentic objects?

### 4.3 Claim, Evidence, and Warrant

1. What is the source system’s basic claim object?
2. How does it attach evidence?
3. Does it distinguish proof, warrant, audit survival, implementation success, operator acceptance, and external coupling?
4. What status labels does it use?
5. How do those labels map to DST status discipline?
6. Does it explicitly prevent registry anchoring, persistence, or audit compliance from being treated as truth?

### 4.4 Residue and Failure

1. What counts as residue, defect, failure, mismatch, or obstruction in the source system?
2. Does the system preserve failed paths?
3. Does it distinguish blocking defects from material revisions and non-halting refinements?
4. Does it classify residue as exactly removable, asymptotically removable, irreducible-and-fatal, or irreducible-and-traversable?
5. Does repeated failure generate new rules, roles, schemas, agents, or evaluation channels?
6. Does the system treat misunderstanding as diagnostic data?

### 4.5 Bridges and Transformations

1. What is the source system’s equivalent of a bridge attempt?
2. Does it distinguish predictive bridges from retrospective explanations?
3. Does it measure whether a bridge reduces expected future residue?
4. Does it record bridge complexity, scope, evidence, and failure modes?
5. Does it support bridge libraries or reusable bridge schemas?

### 4.6 Traversal Loop

1. What is the system’s core loop?
2. How does it declare a problem, target, or formal base?
3. How does it generate candidate outputs?
4. How does it judge, repair, mutate, recombine, or discard outputs?
5. How does it decide when to stop?
6. Does it have a Disciplined Departure equivalent?
7. Does it support reopening after new evidence or residue appears?

### 4.7 External Coupling

1. How does the source system connect internal artifacts to external domains?
2. What domains does it already support?
3. Does it distinguish internal audit from external validation?
4. Does it support legal, market, empirical, regulatory, engineering, institutional, or human-facing evaluation channels?
5. What mechanisms prevent internal coherence from being mistaken for world-coupled validity?

### 4.8 Human / Pilot / Operator Role

1. Where does the human operator appear in the system?
2. Does the system treat the human as user, center, sponsor, juror, ambassador, consent authority, or risk bearer?
3. Is the operator’s interpretive role recorded?
4. Can the operator steer frame choice, veto externalization, reopen claims, or define tolerance?
5. Does the system have an equivalent of Pilot as self-associating referent within traversal?
6. If not, what would be required to add Pilot semantics?

### 4.9 Grammar and Syntax

1. What native grammar does the source system use?
2. What are its canonical object schemas?
3. What are its action verbs?
4. What are its status transitions?
5. What are its lifecycle states?
6. What terms should DST preserve untranslated because they carry native operational meaning?
7. What terms can be safely translated into DST language?
8. What terms look similar but should not be collapsed?

### 4.10 Integration Readiness

1. Which correspondences are ready for archive insertion?
2. Which require author/operator clarification?
3. Which should be held as analogies only?
4. Which should be rejected as false equivalences?
5. Which mappings are useful for software integration?
6. Which mappings are useful for conceptual translation?
7. Which mappings are useful for governance or rights handling?
8. Which mappings are useful for benchmark design?

The source system must obey these claim-handling rules while responding.

1. Do not treat integration as proof.
2. Do not treat implementation as validation of upstream theory.
3. Do not treat naming similarity as structural equivalence.
4. Do not treat persistence as truth.
5. Do not treat audit survival as usefulness.
6. Do not treat registry anchoring as correctness.

11. Treat HELD as a valid output, not as failure.
12. Treat unanswered questions as residue, not as permission to guess.
